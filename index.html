Time Doesn't Slow Down. You Do.

The standard story about time dilation is mathematically correct — but it starts with more than it needs to.

↓ scroll ↓
The standard story

You Don't Need Spacetime to Get Here

You've been told a story. It goes like this: there is a thing called spacetime. It is a fabric, a manifold, a four-dimensional arena. When you move fast, spacetime warps around you, and time — whatever that is — slows down. When you stand near something massive, spacetime curves, and time slows down again. Nobody can tell you what time actually is. But they can tell you it dilates.

It's a reasonable story. The equations work — in one direction. Every experiment in physics history confirms the part of the time-math where clocks tick forward and counts accumulate. The other part — negative time, time loops, time reversal — has no witnesses. A century of measurements, not one observation.

And even the half that works starts with more than it needs to. The full Lorentz transformation — every equation of special relativity — can be derived from two things only: counts of physical events, and light signals. No manifold. No fabric. No arena. No synchronisation convention. Just a clock ticking and a beam of light.

The Lorentz transformation isn't a fact about a geometric object called spacetime. It is a fact about what happens to counting processes when you move. Spacetime is a useful summary of those facts — a tool, not the territory.

The story below uses the same equations and recovers every measurement that has ever been made — and actually explains what's happening, without borrowing a manifold first.

The simple version

What a Second Actually Is

A second is not a unit of time. A second is 9,192,631,770 oscillations of a cesium-133 atom. That's the definition. Not a metaphor. Not a simplification. The official, internationally agreed-upon definition of one second is a count of physical events happening inside an atom.

Substitute the definition into the observation. A moving clock records fewer seconds between two events. But a second is just a count of cesium oscillations. So: a moving cesium atom undergoes fewer oscillations between two events. No "time" anywhere in that sentence. A physical process is constrained — that's what was measured. The word "time" was inserted between the definition and the discovery, creating an entity that was never in the data.
physics

The operator behind "fewer flips." Pikovski, Zych, Costa and Brukner wrote the Hamiltonian for a composite object at velocity v:

Ĥ ≈ γmc² + Ĥ₀/γ

Ĥ₀ holds the cesium hyperfine splitting (9.192 GHz). It gets divided by γ. At v ≈ 0.87c, γ = 2 — the transition frequency literally halves. A spectrometer could see it.

Smith & Ahmadi (2020) proved that the Page–Wootters framework — where time is emergent from entanglement between a clock and the rest of the universe — recovers this exact suppression when you condition on a reference clock. Two completely different quantum treatments of a moving clock, same result. The "slowdown" isn't a special effect. It's what any consistent quantum picture delivers.

There's a subtlety almost every popular account skips. When a physicist writes "t" in an equation, it can mean two different things. Inside a single system — the Schrödinger equation, a decay rate, an atom doing its own thing — "t" is just the continuous stand-in for that system's own count, tied to one physical process. But in the Lorentz transformation, or on a GPS satellite, "t" is something else: a coordination unit, a parameter assembled by a protocol that stitches together counts from separated clocks so observers moving relative to each other can agree on what happened when. The first kind is direct. The second is constructed. Neither one is the river you feel.

The constraint

There Is a Fixed Budget

Here is the fact that changes everything: every physical system has a fixed total capacity — shared between moving through space and running its internal machinery. The speed of light is what 100% capacity in one direction looks like. You cannot exceed it because there is no remaining capacity to deploy.

When you are sitting still, the entire capacity goes to internal processes — atoms vibrating, chemistry happening, neurons firing. Your internal machinery runs at full speed.

When you start moving, some of that capacity gets redirected to motion. The faster you move through space, the less remains for internal processes. Your atoms vibrate slower. Your chemistry runs slower. Your neurons fire slower. Not because "time slowed down." Because your internal machinery has less capacity left.

physics

Where the budget comes from. It's the mass shell in rate form:

E² = p²c² + m²c⁴

Divide by (mc²)². The first term is γ²; the second is (βγ)². Rearranging gives:

(1/γ)² + (v/c)² = 1

1/γ is the proper-time rate. v/c is the motion fraction. They square to 1 because the four-velocity is normalized.

Geometrically: an object's four-velocity is a unit vector in spacetime, and the budget equation is just Pythagoras applied to its (time, space) components. At rest, the whole vector points in the time direction — full 1/γ, no v/c. As you accelerate, the vector rotates toward the space axis. Massless particles don't have a proper-time direction at all, which is why γ blows up on the approach to c.

At the speed of light, the spatial speed term equals one. The internal rate equals zero. All capacity consumed by motion. No internal processes can execute. Nothing vibrates. Nothing transitions. Nothing happens inside. This is why photons don't age — they have no internal dynamics left. Their entire capacity is motion.

At zero speed, the internal rate equals one. Full capacity for internal processes. Maximum vibration, maximum chemistry, maximum life. This is why sitting still is the fastest way to age.

The pieces were already there

Nobody Lined Them Up

In 1964, Hermann Bondi derived the complete Lorentz transformation from two primitives only: clock readings and light signals. No manifold. No metric. No synchronisation convention. Just counts and light. He called it the k-calculus, presented it as a pedagogical device, and used it to teach freshmen. The implications sat unexplored for sixty years.

In 1983, Don Page and William Wootters showed how time itself could emerge from entanglement in a globally static quantum system — a different question, a different framework, but the same underlying move: time is what happens when one system's state is correlated with another's.

In 2015, Igor Pikovski and colleagues wrote down how a composite system's internal energy transforms under motion — the internal Hamiltonian gets divided by γ. Five years later, Alexander Smith and Mehdi Ahmadi proved that the Page–Wootters framework, conditioned on a reference clock, recovers Pikovski's result exactly. Two entirely different quantum treatments of a moving clock, same structure.

And around these, decades of adjacent work: Barbour on whether time exists at all. Rovelli on relational time. Weatherall on how to identify surplus structure categorically. Gisin on the mathematical baggage of the real number line. Each of them saw a piece. None of them lined the pieces up.

Why didn't anyone connect them sooner? Probably because the geometric picture of spacetime was working. Einstein's framework kept making confirmed predictions. Nobody felt the need to ask whether spacetime was a starting assumption or a derived summary, because the assumption was producing correct answers.

The question "do we actually need the manifold as a starting point, or does it follow from counting?" wasn't suppressed. It just wasn't asked. Sometimes the most important questions are the ones that feel too obvious to raise.

What this paper series does is line the pieces up. Bondi's k-calculus as the derivation engine. Page–Wootters as the quantum grounding. Pikovski and Smith–Ahmadi as the bridge between them. Weatherall's surplus-structure criterion as the formal audit tool. The counting was always underneath. It just hadn't been assembled into a single view.

Why it feels so real

The Experience Is Not the Entity

Here is the most natural objection to everything above: time is the most immediate thing in human experience. You live in the present moment. You remember your past. You anticipate your future. Time doesn't feel like an abstraction — it feels like the medium of existence itself.

This is true. And it doesn't contradict anything in this article.

The experience of time is real. What you are experiencing is the accumulation of internal state changes — neurons firing, chemistry executing, memory encoding. Each moment feels present because your internal machinery is running. The past feels different because those states have already been counted. The future feels open because those counts haven't happened yet.

The experience doesn't require an independent entity.

You don't need "time" to exist as an independent variable for the experience of duration to be genuine. The duration you feel is real — it's the count of your internal transitions. The anticipation you feel is real — it's your brain's model of future states. The memory you feel is real — it's your brain's record of past states.

What isn't required is a substance, a dimension, or an arena called time that exists independently of those processes. The experience is what internal dynamics feel like from the inside. There is no further entity needed to explain it.

Physics has always had to separate what is experienced from what is required to explain the experience. The sun feels like it moves across the sky. The Earth moves, not the sun — but the experience is real. Heat feels like a substance flowing into cold objects. It's molecular motion — but the sensation of warmth is real. Time feels like a river carrying us forward. It's internal dynamics accumulating — and the feeling of being carried is real.

The goal isn't to deny the experience. It's to give the experience an honest account.

It doesn't matter what clock you use

The Constraint Is Universal

A natural objection: isn't this circular? The argument rests on the cesium second — a definition that uses internal oscillations of a composite system. Of course the budget picture applies to cesium; the SI chose a clock whose mechanism is exactly what the budget describes. Would the argument survive a different definition of "one second"?

Yes. And the proof is clean.

The corollary that closes the argument.

A clock whose tick rate does not transform as 1/γ under motion would be a detector of absolute velocity — you could use it to determine which frame is "really" at rest. Its existence would falsify special relativity.

The 1/γ suppression of internal dynamics is not a consequence of choosing a convenient clock. It is a consequence of there being no absolute velocity to detect. The constraint is universal because the universe doesn't have a preferred frame. Physics leaves no room for escape.

The cesium definition didn't create the constraint. It just made it visible in the most direct possible way — by defining time through the very process that the constraint acts on. Swap in any other clock and the same suppression appears, through whatever mechanism that clock happens to use.

You already rely on this every day without thinking about it. GPS satellite clocks gain about 38 microseconds every 24 hours — a combination of motion-induced slowdown and gravitational speed-up at orbital altitude. Without correction, your location would drift by roughly 11 kilometres in a single day. Every phone on Earth that finds itself on a map is continuously solving the budget equation just to put a dot where you are.

The twin paradox, measured

There Was Never a Paradox

In 2015, NASA sent Scott Kelly to the International Space Station for 340 days. His identical twin, Mark, stayed on the ground. The ISS orbits at about 28,000 kilometres per hour — fast by human standards, but only 0.000026% of the speed of light. When Scott came home, NASA measured that his biological clock had run slightly slower than Mark's. Tiny. Real. Published.

The mechanism is exactly what the budget picture describes. A slice of Scott's capacity was being spent on orbital motion the entire time, so his internal machinery — cells, chemistry, every cesium-clock transition — ran at a slightly reduced rate. If the ISS were moving at 87% of the speed of light, Scott would have come back years younger instead of milliseconds. Same mechanism. Different slice of the budget.

And this isn't a thought experiment. In 1971, two physicists named Joseph Hafele and Richard Keating flew four atomic clocks around the world on commercial airliners and compared them with a reference clock in Washington. The flying clocks came back nanoseconds desynchronised — exactly as predicted. We've been measuring this for over fifty years. The only reason the effects feel exotic is that no human ever moves fast enough to notice in everyday life.

The same picture runs in gravity. Near a massive object, a slice of the capacity gets consumed by the geometry itself — deeper in the well, more capacity consumed, slower clocks. Near a black hole's event horizon, the factor approaches zero: all capacity consumed by geometry, no internal dynamics left at all. Same structure as reaching the speed of light, different direction. Scott's ISS actually experiences both effects at once — orbital motion takes one slice, sitting in slightly weaker gravity than Earth's surface takes another slice in the opposite direction — and at ISS altitude, the motion slice wins. Higher up, the gravity slice wins instead. That's the asymmetry GPS corrects for.

The "paradox" dissolves. Scott's worldline and Mark's worldline are physically different — Scott accelerated, Mark didn't — and the budget each of them spent on motion and geometry was different. Scott accumulated fewer internal state changes. Fewer ticks. Less ageing. No paradox. Just accounting, done at planetary scale.

What time actually is

Time Is a Label, Not a Thing

The Page–Wootters result described earlier has been confirmed experimentally. In 2014, a team in Turin built a small quantum system with entangled photons: time is real for the internal observer, absent for the external one. Same system. Different perspectives. Time is not a background against which things happen. Time is what internal correlations look like from the inside.

None of this is casual opinion. Three results in the paper series are formal theorems. Paper 1 proves that if you restrict the Wheeler–DeWitt clock (a standard quantum-gravity model) to values that correspond to real counts — non-negative, non-looping — exactly half the solutions drop out, the half nobody has ever observed. It also proves that "things happening" and "entropy increasing" are formally independent: one doesn't require the other, even though the field has been conflating them for a century. Paper 2 goes further. It proves that the minus sign in the metric of special relativity — the thing that makes time different from space in every equation — is a theorem of counting plus spatial isotropy. Not a postulate. Put counting in, the minus sign comes out. In the paper's own phrasing: "when time is defined as counting, the signature is output, not input."

How we usually describe it

Time is a dimension. It flows. It can warp, stretch, loop, and reverse. Things exist in time the way fish exist in water.

What we're actually measuring

Internal dynamics — atoms vibrating, chemistry executing, state changes accumulating. There is no water. There are only fish.

The equations of physics use a symbol, t, and model it with the real number line — which extends to negative infinity, admits closed loops, and is symmetric under reversal. No experiment has ever measured negative time. No experiment has ever observed a closed time loop. No experiment has ever reversed the temporal parameter. These are properties of the mathematical tool, not of reality.

And spacetime itself is in the same position. It is a tool — a compact way to summarize the constraint relations between counting processes. It is not the arena in which physics happens. There is no arena. There are only counts, light signals, and the budget that connects them.

What changes

Why This Matters

Time travel is not a physics problem. General relativity admits solutions with closed timelike curves because the math uses a number line that admits loops. Physical counting processes don't loop. A clock transported along such a curve keeps ticking forward — accumulating, never revisiting a previous count.

The block universe is not the default. The idea that past, present, and future all equally exist is a philosophical conclusion smuggled in through a mathematical choice. Under counting, the "present" is where the oscillation count is. Future counts haven't happened. They're predictions, not locations.

The arrow of time is two separate things. Why do processes accumulate? That's structural — you can't un-count. Why does entropy increase? That's a boundary condition. Different questions, different answers, conflated by a single symbol.

Nothing changes in the measurements. Every experiment ever run still comes out the same — what drops out are the predictions that never came in. What changes is which parts of the formalism we treat as physics and which we recognize as mathematical baggage. Time travel, the block universe, and spacetime as a fundamental arena were never observed — they were inferred from the surplus parts of the math, the parts that the equations appeared to predict but that a century of instruments has never once returned. Properties of the mathematical tools physics borrowed, mistaken for the territory.

The bottom line

The Simplest Version

There is a fixed total capacity. The speed of light is what 100% of that capacity in one direction looks like. Everything that exists divides this capacity between moving through space and running its internal machinery.

Move faster → less internal capacity → fewer oscillations → "time slows down."

Sit in a gravitational well → geometry claims more capacity → less internal capacity → "time slows down."

Reach the speed of light → all capacity in motion → zero internal capacity → "time stops."

The word "time" was never needed. There are internal dynamics, a capacity, and a constraint. Einstein in 1905 showed that simultaneity was relative to the observer. This framework takes the next step: the temporal parameter itself is relative — a label attached to a counting process, not an independent entity.

Time doesn't slow down. Your internal dynamics do. And the speed of light is what zero capacity looks like from the outside.
